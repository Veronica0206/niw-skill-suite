# Vera NIW Entrepreneur

## Purpose

Guide entrepreneur/business founder NIW petitions through the USCIS Policy
Manual's entrepreneur-specific evidentiary framework. Entrepreneur NIW cases
have unique requirements, common traps, and different evidence structures
compared to academic or industry employee petitions.

**Critical context:** The USCIS Policy Manual (Jan 2025 update) explicitly
states: **"Not every entrepreneur qualifies for national interest waiver."**
This skill applies the specific guidance USCIS provides for entrepreneur cases.

**Approval rate context (FY 2024-2025):**
- Overall NIW approval rates: 43-67% (down from 80%+ historically)
- STEM entrepreneurs maintain stronger rates (~90%)
- Non-STEM entrepreneurs face 40-60% denial rates
- Entrepreneur cases face additional scrutiny on financial viability
- Officers increasingly demand concrete U.S.-focused outcomes over abstract claims

---

## Step 0 — Entrepreneur-Specific Screening

Before proceeding, confirm the petitioner is genuinely pursuing an
entrepreneurial NIW (not a standard industry case mislabeled):

**True entrepreneur indicators:**
- Owns or co-founded a business entity
- Is the primary decision-maker or intellectual driver of the venture
- The endeavor IS the business/venture (not work done for a large employer)
- Plans to continue as self-employed or as company principal

**Not an entrepreneur case (route to standard pipeline):**
- Works as an employee at a company, even if "entrepreneurial" in spirit
- Is a consultant at a firm they don't own
- Plans to seek traditional employment after NIW approval

## Step 1 — EB-2 Eligibility Gate (Same as Standard Pipeline)

The EB-2 gate applies identically to entrepreneurs. Confirm:

- [ ] **Advanced Degree route:** Occupation underlying the endeavor requires
  at least a bachelor's degree for standard entry. The petitioner holds a
  relevant advanced degree OR bachelor's + 5 years post-degree experience
  in the **same specialty**.
- [ ] **Exceptional Ability route (alternative):** 3 of 6 criteria met, with
  exceptional ability **directly related** to the proposed endeavor.
  Two-step analysis applies: meeting criteria is necessary but insufficient —
  quality must demonstrate "expertise significantly above ordinarily encountered."

**Entrepreneur-specific EB-2 trap:** Just because someone runs a successful
business doesn't mean they qualify for EB-2. The occupation underlying the
endeavor must require advanced qualifications. A restaurateur with a PhD in
chemistry cannot claim EB-2 for a restaurant endeavor.

## Step 2 — Entrepreneur-Specific Evidence Framework

The USCIS Policy Manual identifies specific evidence categories for
entrepreneur NIW cases. Collect and evaluate each:

### 2.1 Ownership & Central Role (REQUIRED)

- [ ] Ownership interest in U.S. entity (articles of incorporation,
  operating agreement, stock certificates)
- [ ] Appointment as officer or key position (corporate documents)
- [ ] Active, central operational role (not passive investor)

**Without these, the entrepreneur case cannot proceed.** An entrepreneur
who cannot demonstrate ownership and central role has no standing for
an entrepreneur-specific NIW argument.

### 2.2 Background Credentials

- [ ] Relevant degrees, certifications, licenses in the field
- [ ] Track record of successfully leading prior startups or ventures
- [ ] Combination of relevant education + entrepreneurial experience

### 2.3 Investment & Financial Evidence

- [ ] Outside investor funding or binding commitments
- [ ] Angel investor or venture capital participation
- [ ] Capital amounts appropriate to the endeavor's scope
- [ ] Revenue generation and growth evidence
- [ ] Financial projections with realistic implementation timelines

**2025-2026 trend:** Officers increasingly scrutinize financial viability.
Business plans must include detailed implementation timelines and realistic
financial projections — not just market opportunity descriptions.

### 2.4 Incubator/Accelerator Participation

- [ ] Admission to recognized incubator or accelerator program
- [ ] Documentation of the program's track record and selectivity
- [ ] Evidence that admission constitutes endorsement of plan and capability

### 2.5 Intellectual Property

- [ ] Patents with documentation showing field significance
- [ ] Evidence of petitioner's contribution to IP development
- [ ] Internal or external usage documentation
- [ ] Trademarks or copyrights developed by the petitioner

### 2.6 Awards & Government Support

- [ ] Federal, state, or local government economic development support
- [ ] Research/development entity grants (SBIR, STTR, NSF I-Corps, etc.)
- [ ] Policy/research institute awards
- [ ] Government agency letters (especially strong — see Section 4)

### 2.7 Published Materials & Recognition

- [ ] Media articles about the petitioner or the venture
- [ ] Documentation of outlet reputation and reach
- [ ] Industry recognition, awards, speaking invitations

### 2.8 Impact Metrics

- [ ] U.S. job creation numbers (with documentation)
- [ ] Revenue and growth trajectory
- [ ] Location in economically depressed area (if applicable)
- [ ] Customer/user adoption metrics

### 2.9 Third-Party Validation Letters

- [ ] Letters from government entities with knowledge of the work
- [ ] Letters from investors or business associations
- [ ] Industry/technology experts providing peer-review-equivalent assessment
- [ ] Letters from customers or users documenting impact

## Step 3 — Entrepreneur-Specific Failure Modes

Check the endeavor against USCIS Policy Manual explicit exclusions:

### Trap 1: Consulting in Shortage Occupation
> "Opening a consulting firm in a shortage occupation does not establish
> national importance."

FAILS: Starting an IT consulting firm because there's a shortage of IT workers.
PASSES: Building a specific technology platform that addresses a national problem.

### Trap 2: Industry-Level Importance Claims
> Industry-level statistics (e.g., "car dealerships contribute $X to GDP")
> do not establish the SPECIFIC endeavor's national importance.

FAILS: "The restaurant industry contributes $1 trillion to the U.S. economy."
PASSES: "My specific food safety technology reduces contamination incidents
affecting 48 million annual foodborne illness cases (CDC)."

### Trap 3: General Job Creation
> "General business owner/entrepreneur assertions for job creation" are
> insufficient standing alone.

FAILS: "My company will create 50 jobs."
PASSES: "My company's technology enables X, creating economic value of $Y,
with job creation as one of multiple national benefits."

### Trap 4: Benefits Limited to Specific Employers
> Benefits limited to specific employers don't establish national importance.

FAILS: "My software makes my company more efficient."
PASSES: "My software addresses a systemic problem across the entire industry,
with documented adoption by multiple organizations."

### Trap 5: Startup Without Detailed National Impact
> Startup companies without "detailed national impact explanation" fail Prong 1.

FAILS: "We're building an AI startup."
PASSES: Detailed explanation of specific national problem, specific solution,
quantified national-scale impact, and evidence of progress.

## Step 4 — Government Agency Letters (Highest-Value Evidence)

For entrepreneur cases, government agency letters are especially powerful
because they address the skepticism officers have about business claims.

**Relevant sources for entrepreneurs:**
- SBA district office letters
- Economic development agencies (federal, state)
- SBIR/STTR program officers
- DOE, DOD, or agency program managers (if applicable)
- NSF I-Corps program
- State/local economic development commissions

**What the letter should address:**
- How the venture aligns with the agency's economic development mission
- Specific programs or initiatives the venture supports
- Why the petitioner's specific approach (not just the market) matters
- Urgency: why delay through labor certification would harm outcomes

## Step 5 — Endeavor Statement for Entrepreneurs

Apply the standard 6-ingredient framework from vera-niw-endeavor, with
entrepreneur-specific adjustments:

**Ingredient 1 — Named project:** The venture/company name + specific product/service
**Ingredient 2 — National-scale problem:** NOT "market opportunity" — a genuine
  national problem with quantified scope
**Ingredient 3 — Named methodology:** The specific technical or business innovation
  — NOT "running a company" or "providing consulting services"
**Ingredient 4 — Application domain:** Must be Tier 1-2. Tier 3 entrepreneurs
  face very high denial rates.
**Ingredient 5 — Measurable national-scale outcome:** Revenue and jobs are
  insufficient alone. Must demonstrate broader national benefit.
**Ingredient 6 — Substitutability:** Why this specific founder — what unique
  expertise, IP, or approach do they bring?

**Additional ingredient for entrepreneurs:**
**Ingredient 7 — U.S. operational presence:** Ownership interest, U.S. entity,
  active operations, contracts with American clients, physical presence

**Key standard (USCIS Policy Manual):** The petitioner is **NOT required** to
show the endeavor is "more likely than not to ultimately succeed." However,
they must demonstrate all three Dhanasar prongs by preponderance of evidence.
"Many innovations and entrepreneurial endeavors ultimately fail, despite
intelligent plan and competent execution." Success is not required — but
evidence of serious pursuit is.

## Step 6 — Prong 3 for Entrepreneurs (Special Considerations)

Entrepreneur Prong 3 arguments differ from academic/industry cases:

### 6.1 Impracticality of Labor Certification

**Strongest argument for entrepreneurs:** The petitioner IS the endeavor.
A PERM process requires an employer-employee relationship — but the
petitioner is self-employed. Labor certification is structurally
incompatible with self-directed entrepreneurial work.

- Self-employment inherently doesn't adversely affect U.S. workers
- The venture provides U.S. jobs (positive, not displacement)
- The petitioner's IP, methodology, and vision cannot be sourced through
  a standard hiring process

### 6.2 Benefit Despite Available Workers

- Other qualified workers exist, but THIS specific venture/product/approach
  is uniquely the petitioner's creation
- The value is in the specific innovation, not in filling a position
- Documented evidence of unique IP, methodology, or approach

### 6.3 Urgency

- Time-sensitive funding windows (VC rounds, grants, government contracts)
- Competitive market timing — delay allows competitors to establish
- Ongoing U.S. operations that would be disrupted by departure
- Government program deadlines or initiatives the venture supports
